Circadian misalignment underlies immune escape in breast cancer

Published: March 4, 2026
Status: Under review at Cell

Problem Statement

This research aims to elucidate the circadian programs of immune and tumor cells within the triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) microenvironment. We focus on how circadian regulation influences tumor progression and how the temporal coordination, or lack thereof, between cell types impacts the efficacy of immunotherapy.

Key Findings

  • Temporal Misalignment: Using time-resolved single-nucleus multiomic (snRNA-seq and snATAC-seq) profiling of over 100,000 nuclei, we discovered that tumor and immune cells follow distinct 24-hour cycles that are often out of sync, facilitating immune evasion.
  • Asynchronous Proliferation and Recognition: Cancer cell proliferation peaks during the early morning when anti-tumor immune activity is at its lowest. Furthermore, the timing of tumor antigen presentation is “phase-mismatched” with T-cell recognition, weakening the immune system’s ability to target the cancer.
  • Extended Immune Suppression: Rhythms of PD-1 and PD-L1 are asynchronous. Even when T cells start to recover from the exhausted stage, sustained PD-L1 expression from tumor and myeloid cells extends immune suppression.
  • Chronotherapy Potential: These findings suggest that the timing of chronotherapy can be optimized to align with peak immune activity, potentially improving clinical outcomes.

My Contributions

I applied MetaTIME to annotate cell subtypes and functional states across mouse and human samples. These high-resolution annotations were critical for the downstream analysis of how specific cell states, such as proliferative vs. immunogenic epithelial cells, oscillate in a circadian manner.
